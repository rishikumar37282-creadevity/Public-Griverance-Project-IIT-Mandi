CHAPTER 1 · THE PROBLEM

Every complaint waits in the same line

Government grievance systems receive lakhs of complaints. Triage is manual, so everything queues first-come-first-served. The cost of slowness is asymmetric: a late answer to a suggestion is harmless; a late answer to a stopped pension or an unsafe school roof is not.

“please improve the website”→ “add more counters”→ “elderly mother's pension stopped 8 months, medical bills unpaid”→ “ration card query”→ officer desk 🏛️
The goal: read every complaint the moment it arrives, predict its department and urgency, and let emergencies jump the queue — with a human officer always making the final call.

Three stakeholders, three needs

Trust and improve the model: full observability of training, per-epoch logs, explainable attention, and one-click retraining with any hyperparameters.

Success metric decided on day one: Critical-class recall — never plain accuracy. Missing an emergency is the one error the system must not make.
CHAPTER 2 · THE DATA

4,840 grievances, 15 departments — and one rare class

Derived from the Government of India Grievance Report (Kaggle), joined with the official CPGRAMS category tree (19,853 nodes) for department and sub-category codes. The catch that shaped the whole project: Critical is only 2.4% of the data.

Why this changes everything

A model that never predicts Critical is still 97.6% accurate — and completely useless. So accuracy was banned as the headline metric, the loss was class-weighted, and checkpoints were selected on Critical-recall. Labels come from a transparent weak-supervision oracle: severity keywords + pending durations + vulnerable-group mentions + escalation depth → a 0–100 score → Low / Medium / High / Critical.

CHAPTER 3 · DESIGN DECISION

Why a BiLSTM with attention?

We compared three families before writing a line of training code:

TF-IDF + Linear model

Fast and simple, but bag-of-words: cannot tell “pension not stopped” from “pension stopped”, and offers no token-level explanation.

baseline only

BiLSTM + Attention ✓

Reads word order in both directions, right-sized for 4.8k samples (~435k params), trains in seconds on CPU — and the attention weights give built-in explainability we surface to citizens.

chosen2 parallel heads

Pretrained transformer

Strongest ceiling (MuRIL for Indian languages) but heavy for this dataset size and harder to train/serve in a student setting. Kept as the Tier-2 roadmap.

future work
One model, two answers: a shared encoder with parallel category + urgency heads (multi-task learning) — both tasks share the same understanding of the complaint, and each head's loss regularises the other.

Why bidirectional?

“no response despite repeated complaints” — the urgency of “no response” only becomes clear from what follows it. Reading both ways gives every token full-sentence context.

Why attention?

It compresses variable-length complaints into one vector and tells us which words drove the decision — the same α weights citizens see highlighted in their result.

Why two MLP heads?

Department and urgency are different questions with the same evidence. Two 2-layer heads (BatchNorm+ReLU+dropout) specialise on top of one shared encoder.

CHAPTER 3 · THE CRUCIAL TRICK

A loss function that refuses to ignore emergencies

With Critical at 2.4%, a plain loss lets the optimiser buy accuracy by sacrificing the rare class. We made that trade impossible:

L  =  w_cat · CE(category)  +  w_urg · CE(urgency, class-weights)   where  weight(Critical) ×= critical_boost

Inverse-frequency weights

Each urgency class is weighted by 1/frequency — one Critical example counts roughly as much as forty Medium ones.

critical_boost ×3

An extra multiplier on top, tuned in the Training Studio. Missing a Critical becomes the most expensive mistake the model can make.

Checkpoint rule

Best epoch = highest (Critical-recall, macro-F1) — the tie-break stops a degenerate “everything is Critical” epoch from winning.

CHAPTER 4 · TRAINING

The training protocol

✂️

70 / 15 / 15 split

Stratified on urgency so each split keeps the same 2.4% of Critical — essential for a rare class.

⚙️

AdamW + plateau LR

lr 1e-3, weight decay 1e-5, gradient clipping at 5.0. ReduceLROnPlateau halves the LR when val Critical-recall stalls.

🛑

Early stopping

Patience of 10 epochs on Critical-recall; the best checkpoint is restored, never the last one.

📼

Everything recorded

Every epoch of every run → training_log.csv: 68 columns — losses, all metrics, per-class P/R/F1, the 4×4 confusion matrix, and the full hyperparameter snapshot.

Reproducibility rule: if it isn't in the CSV, it didn't happen. That log powers the Experiments dashboard, the training replay — and every chart on the next two slides.

Twisting the knobs — what actually changed the results

Every experiment was launched from the Training Studio and logged. Three tell the story best:

The keeper — zero missed emergencies, strong overall quality.

Crit-recall0.941

Better F1 — but ≈6% of emergencies missed. Exactly the trade we refuse.

Crit-recall1.000
macro-F10.674

Too small + over-regularised → underfits the middle classes.

The lesson: the class-weighted loss is worth ~2 points of macro-F1 and buys 100% emergency capture. Capacity below ~128 hidden units underfits; the notebook defaults were close to optimal.
CHAPTER 5 · REFINEMENT

How the system was refined, iteration by iteration

  1. Found: checkpointing on Critical-recall alone let an epoch that predicts everything Critical win (recall 1.0, F1 0.14). → Fixed: macro-F1 tie-break in checkpoint selection.
  2. Found: plain accuracy looked great (97%+) while the model ignored Critical. → Fixed: headline metric switched to Critical-recall everywhere — dashboards, logs, early stopping.
  3. Found: tuning without records is guessing. → Fixed: 68-column per-epoch CSV log with auto schema migration; the Experiments tab diffs any runs side-by-side.
  4. Found: a black-box "Critical" label wouldn't be trusted by officers or citizens. → Fixed: attention highlights + a rule-based 0–100 score breakdown shown with every prediction.
  5. Found: predictions alone don't help a user act. → Fixed: escalation engine mapping urgency → routing level, SLAs, escalation path and next steps.
Each iteration is visible in the logs — open the Observatory's Experiments tab and replay any run in “Magic of the Architecture · Act 4”.

CHAPTER 8 · HONESTY

Limitations & responsible use

🌐

English-only, 15 departments

Hindi/regional or code-mixed complaints and unseen topics degrade accuracy; unknown words fall back to <UNK>. MuRIL is the Tier-2 answer.

🏷️

Weak-supervision labels

Urgency labels come from a transparent rule oracle, not triage experts. Real deployment needs officer-verified labels and a feedback loop.

👤

Human in the loop

Predictions order the queue; they never close, reject or auto-decide a complaint. Officers make every final call.

🚨

Not an emergency channel

The UI repeatedly says it: for danger to life, call 112. An AI queue must never gate emergency services.

🔒

Privacy by design

Anonymous filing allowed; contact details never returned by the public tracking endpoint; nothing leaves the local server.

⚖️

Deliberate bias — disclosed

The loss prefers false Critical alarms over missed emergencies. That bias is a design choice, documented and tunable.

CHAPTER 9 · WHAT WE LEARNED

The learnings that outlast the project

  1. Pick the metric before the model. Choosing Critical-recall on day one shaped the loss, the checkpointing, the dashboards — everything.
  2. Class imbalance is a design problem, not a nuisance. Weighted loss + boost + stratified splits + recall-based early stopping — each alone is insufficient.
  3. Explainability is a feature, not a paper section. The attention layer earns citizen trust and doubles as a debugging tool.
  4. Log everything, always. The 68-column epoch log turned “I think it's better” into “run A beats run B by 0.034 macro-F1 at equal recall”.
  5. The last mile is the product. SLAs, escalation paths, reference IDs and tracking are what make a classifier useful to a citizen.
